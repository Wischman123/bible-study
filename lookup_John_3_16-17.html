Commentary Comparison

Tyndale Open Study Notes · Jamieson-Fausset-Brown Bible Commentary · John Gill's Exposition of the Whole Bible · Matthew Henry's Complete Commentary

Points of Agreement

Tyndale's perspective establishes several key points that find strong consensus among all commentaries. The study notes emphasize that spiritual rebirth is essential for salvation and must come "from above" through God's power rather than human effort. Jesus' discourse with Nicodemus demonstrates that religious knowledge and moral respectability are insufficient - even elite religious leaders need complete spiritual transformation.

All commentaries converge on this fundamental necessity of regeneration. Jamieson-Fausset-Brown reinforces this with their statement that "no fallen man is or can be spiritual without a regenerating operation of the Holy Ghost." John Gill extensively supports this view, explaining that regeneration is necessary because humans are "carnal and corrupt" by nature. Matthew Henry agrees that "regeneration is absolutely necessary to our happiness here and hereafter" because "it is impossible that we should be happy if we be not holy."

The commentaries also agree on the universality of God's love expressed in John 3:16. Tyndale notes that "God's love extends beyond the limits of race and nation," while the other commentators affirm this scope, though they differ in their specific interpretations of "the world."

All four sources concur that Jesus' divine authority sets him apart from all other teachers, including John the Baptist. They unanimously present the progression from John's ministry to Jesus' supremacy as divinely ordained rather than competitive.

Points of Difference

Tyndale's approach maintains a straightforward evangelical interpretation focused on practical application for modern believers. The study notes present regeneration as a clear theological concept without extensive debate about its mechanics, and interpret "the world" in 3:16 as God's universal love extending to all people.

Jamieson-Fausset-Brown diverges by offering more detailed theological analysis, particularly regarding the metaphor of wind and Spirit, and provides extensive typological interpretation of the bronze serpent. John Gill differs significantly in his interpretation of "the world," arguing it refers specifically to elect Gentiles rather than humanity universally, reflecting his Reformed theological stance. He also provides much more extensive rabbinical background and Hebrew language analysis.

Matthew Henry takes a different approach by focusing heavily on practical moral applications and pastoral concerns. His commentary emphasizes the experiential aspects of faith and includes more detailed character analysis of Nicodemus's motivations and fears.

The commentaries also differ in their handling of water baptism in verse 5. Tyndale briefly notes the connection between John's baptism and Jesus' Spirit baptism. Gill explicitly rejects baptismal regeneration, while Jamieson-Fausset-Brown and Henry discuss the sacramental significance more positively, though stopping short of endorsing regenerative baptism.

Unique Contributions

Tyndale's unique strength lies in its accessibility and practical focus. The study notes provide clear, modern explanations without overwhelming technical detail. They uniquely emphasize the narrative flow and literary themes, noting how John uses misunderstanding as a teaching strategy and highlighting the symbolic significance of night and darkness throughout the Gospel. Tyndale also uniquely addresses modern translation debates, such as where Jesus' speech ends and John's commentary begins.

Jamieson-Fausset-Brown uniquely provides extensive typological interpretation, particularly their detailed comparison between the bronze serpent and Christ's crucifixion, offering numerous parallel points that the others barely mention. They also uniquely emphasize the paradoxical language about Christ being simultaneously "Son of man" and "in heaven."

John Gill offers unparalleled rabbinical and historical context, providing extensive Jewish background that illuminates the cultural setting. His detailed discussion of Jewish purification rituals, his identification of Nicodemus with historical Jewish figures, and his extensive quotations from Talmudic sources provide insights unavailable in the other commentaries. He also uniquely addresses Jewish expectations about the Messiah's relationship to Gentiles.

Matthew Henry uniquely focuses on pastoral and devotional applications. His commentary provides the most extensive character analysis of Nicodemus, considering various motives for his nighttime visit. Henry also uniquely emphasizes the emotional and relational aspects of faith, particularly in discussing John the Baptist's joy at Christ's success and the friendship metaphor in the bridegroom passage.

Historical & Theological Context

Tyndale's modern evangelical study Bible approach reflects contemporary evangelical consensus, designed for general Bible study rather than academic or pastoral training. The notes assume basic Christian commitment and focus on clear doctrinal understanding and practical application. This represents the democratization of biblical interpretation, making scholarly insights accessible to lay readers.

Jamieson-Fausset-Brown, written in the 19th century, reflects the classical Reformed tradition with strong emphasis on systematic theology and detailed exegesis. Their approach shows the influence of Scottish Presbyterian scholarship, with careful attention to doctrinal precision and extensive cross-referencing of Scripture. The commentary assumes educated readers familiar with theological terminology.

John Gill represents 18th-century Particular Baptist theology with strong Calvinist commitments. His extensive use of rabbinical sources reflects the scholarly methods of his era, when Christian theologians regularly engaged Jewish interpretation. His detailed discussions of election and predestination show his theological tradition's emphasis on God's sovereignty in salvation.

Matthew Henry embodies Puritan pastoral theology from the early 18th century. His commentary reflects the Puritan emphasis on practical godliness, experiential religion, and the application of Scripture to daily Christian living. His approach assumes a largely Christian audience and focuses on spiritual formation rather than evangelistic apologetics. The detailed moral applications reflect the Puritan concern for holy living and the careful examination of one's spiritual state.

Study Questions

Challenge assumptions, probe reasoning, expose tensions

Challenge Assumptions
  1. Nicodemus assumes that good works and religious knowledge qualify him for God's kingdom - what does Jesus' response reveal about the inadequacy of human achievement?
  2. John the Baptist's disciples assume that competition threatens ministry success - how does John's response challenge our assumptions about kingdom advancement?
  3. Many assume that God's love means absence of judgment - how do verses 16-18 challenge this assumption about divine love?
  4. What assumptions about spiritual understanding does Jesus challenge when he questions Nicodemus about being 'the teacher of Israel' (v. 10)?
Probe Reasoning
  1. If being 'born again' is necessary to see God's kingdom (v. 3), what does this imply about the spiritual condition of unregenerate humanity?
  2. John the Baptist claims 'no one receives his testimony' (v. 32) about Jesus, yet people are following Jesus - how do you account for this apparent contradiction?
  3. If God sent his Son not to condemn but to save the world (v. 17), why does Jesus immediately speak of condemnation in verse 18?
  4. Jesus says the wind 'blows where it wishes' to describe the Spirit's work (v. 8) - what does this suggest about human control over spiritual transformation?
Expose Tensions
  1. How do you reconcile the universal invitation 'whoever believes' (v. 16) with Jesus' teaching that spiritual birth is entirely God's sovereign work (v. 8)?
  2. What tension exists between Nicodemus coming to Jesus seeking truth and Jesus' claim that people generally 'love darkness rather than light' (v. 19)?
  3. If eternal life is received through belief (v. 16), why does verse 36 suggest that disobedience results in God's wrath remaining?
  4. John the Baptist expresses joy at decreasing while Jesus increases (v. 30), but his disciples are troubled by Jesus' success - what does this reveal about different responses to God's will?

Observe → Interpret → Apply

Observe
  1. What specific titles and descriptions are given to Jesus throughout this chapter, and who uses each one?
  2. Map the progression of Nicodemus's questions and responses from verse 2 through verse 9 - what changes?
  3. What contrasts does the text establish between earthly and heavenly realities?
  4. How does the author transition from the Nicodemus conversation to the John the Baptist section (vv. 22-36)?
Interpret
  1. Why does Jesus use the metaphor of wind to explain spiritual birth, and what specific aspects of wind relate to the Spirit's work?
  2. What does Jesus mean by distinguishing between 'earthly things' and 'heavenly things' in verse 12?
  3. How does John the Baptist's bridegroom analogy (v. 29) illuminate the relationship between himself, Jesus, and believers?
  4. What is the connection between believing in the Son and 'obeying' the Son in verse 36?
Apply
  1. What religious or intellectual barriers might prevent someone today from understanding Jesus' message about spiritual rebirth?
  2. How should John the Baptist's example of joyful decrease (v. 30) shape our response when others receive recognition we desire?
  3. In what practical ways can believers 'come to the light' (v. 21) in their daily decisions and relationships?
  4. How does understanding God's motivation for sending Jesus (v. 17) change how we approach others who don't yet believe?

Six cognitive levels from recall to creation

Remember
  1. What Old Testament event does Jesus reference in verse 14, and what specific details does he mention?
  2. List the three times Jesus uses 'Truly, truly' in his dialogue with Nicodemus and what follows each declaration.
  3. What geographical locations are mentioned in the baptism section (vv. 22-24)?
Understand
  1. Explain the dual meaning of 'born again/born from above' and why this would confuse Nicodemus.
  2. Summarize John the Baptist's argument for why he should decrease while Jesus increases.
  3. What does Jesus mean when he says people 'loved darkness rather than light' in verse 19?
Apply
  1. How would you explain spiritual rebirth to someone who, like Nicodemus, thinks only in physical terms?
  2. What would it look like to apply John the Baptist's joy in another's success (v. 29) in your workplace or ministry?
  3. How can believers practically demonstrate that their 'works have been carried out in God' (v. 21)?
Analyze
  1. Compare and contrast Nicodemus's approach to Jesus with that of John the Baptist's disciples - what different concerns drive each group?
  2. Examine the structure of verses 16-21: how do the concepts of love, belief, condemnation, and light/darkness interconnect?
  3. What is the relationship between Jesus' wind metaphor (v. 8) and his later distinction between flesh and spirit (v. 6)?
Evaluate
  1. Assess whether Jesus' seemingly harsh response to Nicodemus in verse 10 is justified given Nicodemus's position and questions.
  2. Judge the validity of John the Baptist's claim that 'no one receives his [Jesus'] testimony' (v. 32) in light of the disciples who were following Jesus.
  3. Evaluate the tension between God's universal love (v. 16) and the reality of condemnation (v. 18) - how do these truths coexist?
Create
  1. Design a modern parable that captures the same truth Jesus was teaching with his wind/Spirit analogy in verse 8.
  2. Compose a brief dialogue between a contemporary religious leader and Jesus that parallels the Nicodemus conversation but addresses modern spiritual misconceptions.
  3. Develop a practical framework for churches to embody both God's love (v. 16) and the urgency of the gospel (v. 36) in their evangelistic approach.

Academic approach: genre, history, language, structure

Genre & Structure
  1. How does John's narrative technique of contrasting dialogues (Nicodemus vs. John the Baptist's response) serve his theological purposes in this chapter?
  2. What structural elements connect the Nicodemus pericope (vv. 1-15) with the universal statements about salvation (vv. 16-21)?
  3. How does the transitional section about baptism (vv. 22-24) function literarily to introduce John the Baptist's final testimony?
Historical Context

Commentary

Jamieson-Fausset-Brown Bible Commentary
Robert Jamieson, A.R. Fausset, David Brown
NIGHT INTERVIEW OF NICODEMUS WITH JESUS. (John 3:1-21) Nicodemus--In this member of the Sanhedrim sincerity and timidity are seen struggling together.
Verse 16
For God so loved, &c.--What proclamation of the Gospel has been so oft on the lips of missionaries and preachers in every age since it was first uttered? What has sent such thrilling sensations through millions of mankind? What has been honored to bring such multitudes to the feet of Christ? What to kindle in the cold and selfish breasts of mortals the fires of self-sacrificing love to mankind, as these words of transparent simplicity, yet overpowering majesty? The picture embraces several distinct compartments: "THE WORLD"--in its widest sense--ready "to perish"; the immense "LOVE OF GOD" to that perishing world, measurable only, and conceivable only, by the gift which it drew forth from Him; THE GIFT itself--"He so loved the world that He gave His only begotten Son," or, in the language of Paul, "spared not His own Son" (Rom 8:32), or in that addressed to Abraham when ready to offer Isaac on the altar, "withheld not His Son, His only Son, whom He loved" (Gen 22:16); the FRUIT of this stupendous gift--not only deliverance from impending "perdition," but the bestowal of everlasting life; the MODE in which all takes effect--by "believing" on the Son. How would Nicodemus' narrow Judaism become invisible in the blaze of this Sun of righteousness seen rising on "the world" with healing in His wings! (Mal 4:2).
Verse 17
not to condemn, &c.--A statement of vast importance. Though "condemnation" is to many the issue of Christ's mission (Joh 3:19), it is not the object of His mission, which is purely a saving one.

Tyndale House Publishers

John Gill's Exposition of the Whole Bible
John Gill
For John was not yet cast into prison. As he afterwards was by Herod, for the sake of Herodias, because he reproved Herod for taking her to be his wife, when she was wife to his brother Philip; see Mat 14:3; and this circumstance shows, that these things were done before that journey of Christ into Galilee, mentioned in Mat 4:12. . John 3:25 joh 3:25 joh 3:25 joh 3:25Then there arose a question,.... A dispute, or controversy, occasioned by the baptism, of John and Christ: between some of John's disciples, and the Jews. The Syriac and Persic versions read, "between one of John's disciples, and a certain Jew"; and Nonnus renders it, "with an Hebrew man"; and so the Alexandrian manuscript; many others read, "with a Jew": the contention between them was about purifying; either about the ceremonial purifications, and ablutions commanded in the law of Moses; or concerning the various washings of persons, and vessels, according to the traditions of the elders, which the Jews in common were very tenacious of; and which they thought were brought into neglect, and contempt, by the baptism of John: and this seems to have been occasioned by the baptism of Christ; which the Jew might improve against the disciple of John, and urge, that since another, besides his master, had set up baptizing, who could tell which was most right and safest to follow? and therefore it would have been much better, if no such rite at all had been used by any, but that the purifications required by the law of Moses, and by their elders, had been strictly and solely attended to.
Verse 16
For God sent not his Son into the world,.... God did send his Son into the world in the likeness of sinful flesh, being made of a woman, and made under the law; and which is an instance of his great love, and not of any disrespect to his Son, or of any inequality between them: but then this was not to condemn the world; even any part of it, or any in it: not the Gentiles, as the Jews thought he would; for though God had suffered them to walk in their own ways, and had winked at, or overlooked the times of their ignorance, and had sent no prophet unto them, nor made any revelation of his will, or any discovery of his special grace unto them; yet he sent his Son now, not to destroy them for their idolatry, and wickedness, but to be the Saviour of them: nor the Jews; for as impenitent and unbelieving, and as wicked as they were, he did not accuse them to the Father, nor judge and condemn them; he was to come again in power and great glory, when he would take vengeance on them, and cause wrath to come upon them to the uttermost, for their disbelief and rejection of him; but this was not his business now: nor the wicked of the world in general; to judge, and condemn them, will be his work, when he comes a second time, in the day God has appointed to judge the world in righteousness. But the end of his mission, and first coming is, that the world through him might be saved; even the world of the elect in general, whom God determined to save, and has chosen, to obtain salvation by Jesus Christ, and has appointed Christ to be the salvation of; and who being sent, came into the world to seek and save them; and his chosen people among the Gentiles in particular: wherefore he is said to be God's salvation to the ends of the earth: and all the ends of the earth are called upon to look unto him, and be saved by him, Isa 49:6.
Verse 17
He that believeth on him is not condemned,.... Whether Jew or Gentile, because a believer is openly in Christ; and there is no condemnation to those that are in him: and though the sentence of death passed upon all in Adam, and judgment came upon all men to condemnation in him; yet this sentence being executed on Christ, the surety of his people, who has been condemned to death, and has suffered it in their stead, his death is a security to them from all condemnation: and they are delivered by him from the curse and condemnation of the law: and having in conversion openly passed from death to life, they shall never enter into condemnation; and this is the happy case of every one that believes in Christ: but he that believeth not is condemned already. The Persic version renders it, "from the beginning"; he remains under the sentence of condemnation passed in Adam upon him; the law accuses him, and pronounces him guilty before God; he is under the curse of it, and it is a ministration of condemnation and death to him; nor has he any thing to secure him from its charge, curse, and condemnation: this must be understood of one that is a final unbeliever, or that lives, and dies, in a state of impenitence, and unbelief: because he hath not believed in the name of the only begotten Son of God; whom God has sent to be the Saviour of lost sinners, and to deliver them from wrath to come; and there is no other name but his, whereby men can be saved; so that such that do not believe in him, must be damned.
