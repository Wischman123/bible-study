Commentary Comparison

Tyndale Open Study Notes · Jamieson-Fausset-Brown Bible Commentary · John Gill's Exposition of the Whole Bible · Matthew Henry's Complete Commentary

Points of Agreement

Tyndale establishes the foundational understanding that Nicodemus represents humanity's need for spiritual transformation despite religious knowledge and good works. The other commentators strongly agree on this central premise. Jamieson-Fausset-Brown describes Nicodemus as one of those "superficial believers" who needed his entire "edifice of religion" shaken to lay a proper foundation. Gill emphasizes that despite being "a man of the Pharisees" and "a ruler of the Jews," Nicodemus was spiritually ignorant of regeneration. Matthew Henry notes the irony that "not many of the rulers, or of the Pharisees" are called, "yet some are, and here was one."

All commentators unanimously agree on the absolute necessity of the new birth. Tyndale states that "to experience spiritual rebirth, a person must be completely renewed through God's power." This aligns perfectly with Jamieson-Fausset-Brown's assertion that regeneration involves "his manner of thinking, feeling, and acting, with reference to spiritual things, undergoing a fundamental and permanent revolution." Gill concurs that without being "regenerated by the Spirit of God," one "cannot see the kingdom of God." Matthew Henry emphasizes that "regeneration is absolutely necessary to our happiness here and hereafter."

The commentators also agree that Christ's sacrificial death was prefigured in Moses lifting up the bronze serpent. Tyndale explains that "Jesus was lifted up on the cross so that all people could understand the way of salvation." The historical commentaries provide extensive detail supporting this typological connection, with Gill offering the most comprehensive analysis of the parallels between the bronze serpent and Christ's crucifixion.

Points of Difference

Tyndale takes a more cautious approach to the meaning of "water and Spirit" in verse 5, simply noting that "John the Baptist baptized with water; Jesus baptizes with the Spirit," without definitively connecting this to baptismal water. In contrast, Matthew Henry explicitly discusses both interpretations: the symbolic meaning of water as spiritual cleansing and "the ordinance of baptism." Gill takes a stronger anti-sacramental stance, arguing extensively that "by 'water' is not meant material water, or baptismal water" but rather "the grace of God." Jamieson-Fausset-Brown sees water as representing "thorough spiritual purification by the operation of the Holy Ghost" while acknowledging baptism as "the great visible door of entrance."

Regarding the scope of God's love in verse 16, Tyndale emphasizes that "God's love extends beyond the limits of race and nation" without limiting its extent. Matthew Henry and Jamieson-Fausset-Brown see "the world" as universal in scope. However, Gill provides a detailed argument that "the world" specifically refers to "the Gentiles particularly, and God's elect among them," offering extensive rabbinical sources to support his view that this was directed against Jewish exclusivism rather than affirming universal atonement.

The commentators differ significantly in their treatment of historical and contextual details. Tyndale provides minimal historical background, focusing on theological significance. Gill offers extensive rabbinical parallels and detailed discussions of Jewish customs and locations. Matthew Henry emphasizes practical application and moral instruction. Jamieson-Fausset-Brown maintains a balance between theological exposition and historical context.

Unique Contributions

Tyndale uniquely provides a modern evangelical framework that emphasizes the literary structure and theological themes of John's Gospel. Its distinctive contribution lies in connecting individual passages to broader themes, such as noting that "night also symbolizes the realm of evil, untruth, and unbelief" and linking this to other passages (9:4; 11:10; 13:30). Tyndale also uniquely addresses textual and translation issues, such as clarifying that "born again: Or born from above" and explaining translator decisions about quotation marks in verses 16-21.

Jamieson-Fausset-Brown excels in providing psychological insights into the characters, particularly Nicodemus's mental state and Jesus's strategic teaching methods. Their analysis that Christ's "blunt and curt reply was plainly meant to shake the whole edifice of the man's religion" reveals sophisticated understanding of pedagogical technique. They also offer unique liturgical perspectives, noting how passages have been used in Christian worship and missions.

Gill provides unparalleled rabbinical scholarship, offering extensive quotations from Jewish sources that illuminate the cultural context. His detailed discussion of Jewish purification customs, his identification of potential connections between the biblical Nicodemus and Nicodemon ben Gorion from Jewish literature, and his comprehensive catalog of rabbinical views on the Messiah provide historical depth found nowhere else in these commentaries.

Matthew Henry uniquely emphasizes practical Christian living and pastoral application. His discussion of how believers should handle success and failure, his analysis of ministerial jealousy through John the Baptist's response, and his focus on moral instruction make his commentary distinctively devotional. He alone extensively addresses the practical implications of doctrine for daily Christian experience.

Historical & Theological Context

Tyndale represents modern evangelical scholarship with its study Bible format designed for contemporary readers. It reflects 21st-century evangelical priorities: theological accuracy, practical application, and accessibility for lay readers. The notes are deliberately concise and focused on helping modern Christians understand ancient texts within their theological framework. This approach prioritizes clarity and practical understanding over comprehensive historical detail.

Jamieson-Fausset-Brown (19th century) reflects the Protestant Reformed tradition with its emphasis on systematic theology and careful exegesis. Writing in the era of higher criticism, they maintain conservative positions while engaging scholarly questions. Their work demonstrates the 19th-century evangelical emphasis on detailed verse-by-verse exposition combined with doctrinal precision.

Gill (18th century) represents Particular Baptist theology with its strong emphasis on divine sovereignty and election. Writing during the height of Protestant scholasticism, Gill demonstrates encyclopedic learning and reflects the 18th-century fascination with ancient languages and customs. His extensive rabbinical quotations reflect the period's belief that Jewish sources were essential for understanding the New Testament context.

Matthew Henry (late 17th/early 18th century) embodies Puritan practical theology with its focus on Christian living and pastoral care. Writing for ministers and educated laypeople, Henry emphasizes moral application and spiritual growth. His work reflects the Puritan integration of doctrinal precision with passionate devotional application, characteristic of post-Reformation English Protestantism.

Study Questions

Challenge assumptions, probe reasoning, expose tensions

Challenge Assumptions
  1. Nicodemus assumes his religious status and recognition of Jesus' miracles qualify him for spiritual insight - what does Jesus' response reveal about the inadequacy of external religious credentials?
  2. John's disciples assume that popularity and numbers indicate spiritual success - how does John the Baptist's response challenge this common assumption about ministry effectiveness?
  3. Many assume that being born into a religious family or culture provides spiritual life - how does Jesus' teaching about the necessity of new birth challenge this presumption?
  4. If God is love (v. 16), why isn't everyone automatically saved - what assumptions about divine love does this passage force us to reconsider?
Probe Reasoning
  1. Jesus moves from Nicodemus' question about signs to teaching about new birth - what logical connection exists between miraculous works and spiritual transformation that Nicodemus missed?
  2. If being born again is necessary to see God's kingdom (v. 3), how can someone who isn't yet born again even understand or desire this new birth - what does this suggest about human spiritual ability?
  3. Jesus says people love darkness because their deeds are evil (v. 19) - but how can someone choose light if they naturally love darkness? What does this reveal about the nature of human choice and divine grace?
  4. John the Baptist claims he can only receive what's given from heaven (v. 27) - how does this principle apply to both ministry success and spiritual transformation, and what does it imply about human responsibility?
Expose Tensions
  1. How do we reconcile the universal scope of God's love for 'the world' (v. 16) with Jesus' earlier statement that 'you do not receive our testimony' (v. 11) - is God's love effective or merely offered?
  2. What tension exists between the sovereign work of the Spirit (like wind that 'blows where it wishes' in v. 8) and human responsibility to believe and obey (implied throughout the passage)?
  3. If Jesus came not to condemn but to save (v. 17), how can people be 'condemned already' (v. 18) - does this mean condemnation existed before Christ's coming, and if so, how is his mission truly salvific?
  4. How do we balance the necessity of being born 'of water and Spirit' (v. 5) with salvation by faith alone - what role do external acts versus internal transformation play in entering God's kingdom?

Observe → Interpret → Apply

Observe
  1. What specific titles and descriptions are given to Jesus by Nicodemus (v. 2), by Jesus himself (v. 13), and by John the Baptist (vv. 28-29)?
  2. List all the metaphors and analogies Jesus uses in this passage (birth, wind, serpent, light/darkness, bridegroom) and note what each is meant to explain.
  3. What questions does Nicodemus ask, and how do they reveal his level of understanding throughout the conversation?
  4. Chart the geographic movements mentioned in verses 22-24 and explain what activities are taking place in each location.
Interpret
  1. Why does Jesus respond to Nicodemus' compliment about his miraculous signs (v. 2) by immediately talking about being born again (v. 3)?
  2. What is the relationship between 'seeing' the kingdom (v. 3) and 'entering' the kingdom (v. 5), and why does Jesus make this distinction?
  3. How does the wind analogy in verse 8 help explain the nature of spiritual rebirth to someone like Nicodemus?
  4. What does the comparison between earthly and heavenly authority mean in verses 31-32, and how does it resolve the disciples' concern about Jesus' growing popularity?
Apply
  1. Nicodemus came to Jesus 'by night' - what fears or social pressures might keep someone today from openly seeking spiritual truth?
  2. How can Jesus' teaching about the necessity of spiritual rebirth inform how we share the gospel with religious people who think they're already right with God?
  3. John the Baptist's joy in decreasing so Christ could increase (vv. 29-30) challenges our natural self-promotion - how can this attitude transform our relationships and service?
  4. Given that people 'loved darkness rather than light because their works were evil' (v. 19), how should this affect our approach to evangelism and confronting sin?

Six cognitive levels from recall to creation

Remember
  1. What was Nicodemus' position among the Jews according to verse 1, and what time of day did he come to Jesus?
  2. Recite the exact words of verse 16 and identify what John the Baptist said about his relationship to Christ in verse 28.
  3. What Old Testament event does Jesus reference in verse 14, and what was John the Baptist doing in the Judean countryside according to verses 23-24?
Understand
  1. Explain why Nicodemus was confused about being 'born again' and how Jesus' explanation about flesh and spirit addressed his misunderstanding.
  2. Paraphrase Jesus' argument in verses 11-12 about earthly versus heavenly things and why Nicodemus struggled to accept his testimony.
  3. Summarize the dispute that arose in verses 25-26 and how it led to John the Baptist's teaching about receiving gifts from heaven.
Apply
  1. How would you use Jesus' teaching about being born of water and Spirit (v. 5) to explain baptism and spiritual transformation to a new believer?
  2. Apply John the Baptist's principle 'He must increase, but I must decrease' (v. 30) to a specific situation where you might struggle with pride or recognition.
  3. Using the light and darkness imagery from verses 19-21, how would you help someone understand why they might be resistant to spiritual truth?
Analyze
  1. Compare and contrast how Nicodemus approaches Jesus versus how John the Baptist's disciples approach him - what does this reveal about different types of spiritual seeking?
  2. Analyze the progression of Jesus' revelation to Nicodemus from new birth (v. 3) to his cosmic mission (vv. 16-17) - why does Jesus build his argument this way?
  3. Break down the elements of verse 36 and examine how belief, obedience, eternal life, and God's wrath interconnect in John's theology.
Evaluate
  1. Assess whether Jesus was being harsh or loving when he challenged Nicodemus as 'the teacher of Israel' in verse 10 - defend your position with evidence from the text.
  2. Critique the assumption that God's love (v. 16) means universal salvation in light of the warnings about condemnation and wrath in verses 18 and 36.
  3. Evaluate John the Baptist's response to his disciples' jealousy about Jesus' success - was his approach the most effective way to handle their concerns?
Create
  1. Design a modern parable or analogy that could help someone today understand Jesus' teaching about spiritual birth as effectively as his wind metaphor in verse 8.
  2. Compose a prayer that Nicodemus might have prayed after this encounter, incorporating the key concepts he learned about new birth, God's love, and eternal life.
  3. Develop a brief dialogue between a contemporary religious leader and Jesus, modeling it after the Nicodemus conversation but addressing modern spiritual misconceptions.

Academic approach: genre, history, language, structure

Genre & Structure
  1. How does John structure this chapter to move from private dialogue (vv. 1-21) to public ministry context (vv. 22-36), and what theological development does this progression create?
  2. Analyze the chiastic or parallel structures in Jesus' discourse - how do concepts like birth, belief, and eternal life interconnect throughout the chapter?
  3. What narrative techniques does John use to transition from the Nicodemus encounter to John the Baptist's testimony, and how do these two sections complement each other thematically?
Historical Context
  1. What would Nicodemus' status as both a Pharisee and 'ruler of the Jews' (likely a Sanhedrin member) have meant in first-century Palestine, and why would this make his nighttime visit particularly significant?
  2. How does the geographical setting of John and Jesus baptizing in different locations (vv. 22-24) reflect the historical transition from John's preparatory ministry to Jesus' messianic mission?
  3. What historical background about Jewish purification debates (v. 25) and proselyte baptism practices would illuminate Jesus' teaching about being 'born of water and Spirit'?
  4. How does the reference to John 'not yet being put in prison' (v. 24) help establish the chronological framework of Jesus' early ministry?
Word Studies
  1. Examine the Greek word 'anothen' in verse 3 - how does its dual meaning ('again' and 'from above') create the misunderstanding that drives the conversation with Nicodemus?
  2. Analyze the use of 'pneuma' (spirit/wind) in verse 8 - how does this wordplay in the original language enhance Jesus' teaching about spiritual rebirth?
  3. Study the term 'monogenes' (only begotten/unique) in verse 16 - what does this word convey about Christ's relationship to the Father that other terms could not?
  4. Investigate the verb tenses in John the Baptist's declaration 'He must increase, but I must decrease' (v. 30) - what do they suggest about the timing and nature of this transition?
Literary Features
  1. How does John employ irony in Nicodemus' literal interpretation of spiritual rebirth, and what does this reveal about the gap between human wisdom and divine truth?
  2. Analyze the symbolism of night and day, light and darkness throughout this passage - how does John develop these themes beyond mere temporal markers?
  3. What function do Jesus' 'truly, truly' statements serve in the dialogue structure, and how do they mark theological escalation in his teaching?
  4. Examine the metaphorical language (birth, wind, serpent, bridegroom) - how do these images work together to create a cohesive picture of salvation and spiritual transformation?

Where this fits in God's big story

Salvation Story
  1. How does Jesus' teaching about being 'born again' (vv. 3-8) fulfill God's Old Testament promises about giving his people a new heart and spirit (Ezekiel 36:26-27)?
  2. What does the bronze serpent typology (v. 14) reveal about how God's Old Testament acts of salvation foreshadowed Christ's work on the cross?
  3. How does verse 16's declaration that 'God so loved the world' expand the scope of salvation beyond the Jewish covenant to encompass all humanity?
  4. In what ways does John the Baptist's decreasing ministry (vv. 27-30) represent the transition from the old covenant preparation to new covenant fulfillment?
Christ Connection
  1. How does Jesus' claim to be the one who 'descended from heaven' (v. 13) establish his unique role as the mediator between God and humanity throughout salvation history?
  2. What does Jesus' statement about speaking 'what we know' and bearing witness to 'what we have seen' (v. 11) reveal about his pre-incarnate existence and divine knowledge?
  3. How does the Father giving 'all things into his hand' (v. 35) connect to Christ's role as the ultimate heir and ruler promised throughout Scripture?
  4. In what way does Jesus being 'lifted up' (v. 14) tie together his crucifixion, resurrection, and ascension as one redemptive act?
God's Character
  1. How does God's love motivating the gift of his Son (v. 16) reveal the character of divine love that has been consistent from creation through redemption?
  2. What does the tension between God's desire to save the world (v. 17) and his wrath against unbelief (v. 36) teach us about divine justice and mercy?
  3. How does the Spirit's sovereign work in regeneration, illustrated by the wind analogy (v. 8), demonstrate God's initiative in salvation throughout biblical history?
  4. What does John the Baptist's recognition that gifts come 'from heaven' (v. 27) reveal about God as the ultimate source of all ministry and spiritual effectiveness?

  2. What passage within John 3 feels most challenging or difficult to understand, and why does it create tension for you?

Observation
  1. What do you notice about how Jesus responds differently to Nicodemus' intellectual approach versus John the Baptist's humble acceptance of God's will?
  2. How does the progression from Nicodemus seeking Jesus privately to the public ministry context reveal something about spiritual growth and discipleship?
  3. What patterns do you see in how Jesus uses concrete images (birth, wind, serpent, light) to explain spiritual realities, and why might this teaching method be effective?
Application
  1. Like Nicodemus coming 'by night,' what fears or concerns might be keeping you from being more open about your faith or spiritual questions?
  2. John the Baptist's willingness to decrease so Christ could increase challenges our natural self-promotion - in what specific area of your life do you need to apply this principle?
  3. Jesus distinguishes between loving darkness and coming to the light (vv. 19-21) - what 'darkness' in your own life needs to be brought into God's light?
  4. How does understanding that eternal life comes through believing in Christ (v. 16) rather than religious achievement change your daily approach to spiritual growth?
Prayer
  1. How can you pray for the kind of spiritual humility that allows God to reveal truth, rather than approaching him with your own assumptions and agenda?
  2. What would it look like to pray for those in your life who, like Nicodemus, are religious but may not yet understand the necessity of spiritual rebirth?
  3. How might you pray for the courage to 'come to the light' in areas where you've been hiding or avoiding God's truth?
  4. Based on John the Baptist's example, how can you pray for God to help you decrease so that Christ might increase in your relationships and service?

Observe
  1. What pattern do you notice in Jesus' three-fold use of 'Truly, truly' in verses 3, 5, and 11, and how does the content escalate each time?
  2. How does the dialogue structure between Jesus and Nicodemus shift from verses 1-9 compared to Jesus' monologue in verses 10-21?
  3. What contrasting pairs (light/darkness, belief/unbelief, flesh/spirit) appear throughout this chapter?
Context
  1. Given that Nicodemus was 'a ruler of the Jews' (v. 1), what would his religious background have led him to expect about entering God's kingdom?
  2. How does the timing 'by night' (v. 2) connect to the light/darkness theme that emerges later in verses 19-21?
  3. What significance would the bronze serpent reference (v. 14) have held for a Pharisee like Nicodemus familiar with Numbers 21?
Interpret
  1. When Jesus says 'born of water and the Spirit' (v. 5), how might this connect to both Jewish purification rituals and John the Baptist's ministry mentioned earlier?
  2. What does Jesus mean by 'earthly things' versus 'heavenly things' in verse 12, and why is this distinction crucial for Nicodemus?
  3. How does John the Baptist's statement 'He must increase, but I must decrease' (v. 30) capture the theological transition happening in this chapter?
Connect
  1. How does verse 16's declaration of God's love connect to the broader biblical narrative of God's covenant relationship with humanity?
  2. What parallels exist between the 'lifting up' of the serpent (v. 14) and Jesus' future crucifixion mentioned elsewhere in John's Gospel?
  3. How does this chapter's emphasis on belief and eternal life relate to John's stated purpose for writing his Gospel (John 20:31)?
Evaluate
  1. What tension exists between Jesus' harsh question 'Are you the teacher of Israel and yet you do not understand?' (v. 10) and his patient explanation that follows?
  2. How do we reconcile God's love for the world (v. 16) with the warning about God's wrath remaining (v. 36)?
  3. Is there a contradiction between 'God did not send his Son...to condemn' (v. 17) and the reality that some 'are condemned already' (v. 18)?
Reflect
  1. Like Nicodemus, what religious assumptions or achievements might prevent modern people from understanding spiritual rebirth?
  2. How does John the Baptist's attitude toward decreasing (v. 30) challenge contemporary approaches to ministry and leadership?
  3. In what ways do people today 'love darkness rather than light' (v. 19), and how does this passage address those tendencies?

Commentary

Jamieson-Fausset-Brown Bible Commentary
Robert Jamieson, A.R. Fausset, David Brown
NIGHT INTERVIEW OF NICODEMUS WITH JESUS. (John 3:1-21) Nicodemus--In this member of the Sanhedrim sincerity and timidity are seen struggling together.
Verse 16
For God so loved, &c.--What proclamation of the Gospel has been so oft on the lips of missionaries and preachers in every age since it was first uttered? What has sent such thrilling sensations through millions of mankind? What has been honored to bring such multitudes to the feet of Christ? What to kindle in the cold and selfish breasts of mortals the fires of self-sacrificing love to mankind, as these words of transparent simplicity, yet overpowering majesty? The picture embraces several distinct compartments: "THE WORLD"--in its widest sense--ready "to perish"; the immense "LOVE OF GOD" to that perishing world, measurable only, and conceivable only, by the gift which it drew forth from Him; THE GIFT itself--"He so loved the world that He gave His only begotten Son," or, in the language of Paul, "spared not His own Son" (Rom 8:32), or in that addressed to Abraham when ready to offer Isaac on the altar, "withheld not His Son, His only Son, whom He loved" (Gen 22:16); the FRUIT of this stupendous gift--not only deliverance from impending "perdition," but the bestowal of everlasting life; the MODE in which all takes effect--by "believing" on the Son. How would Nicodemus' narrow Judaism become invisible in the blaze of this Sun of righteousness seen rising on "the world" with healing in His wings! (Mal 4:2).

John 3:25 joh 3:25 joh 3:25 joh 3:25Then there arose a question,.... A dispute, or controversy, occasioned by the baptism, of John and Christ: between some of John's disciples, and the Jews. The Syriac and Persic versions read, "between one of John's disciples, and a certain Jew"; and Nonnus renders it, "with an Hebrew man"; and so the Alexandrian manuscript; many others read, "with a Jew": the contention between them was about purifying; either about the ceremonial purifications, and ablutions commanded in the law of Moses; or concerning the various washings of persons, and vessels, according to the traditions of the elders, which the Jews in common were very tenacious of; and which they thought were brought into neglect, and contempt, by the baptism of John: and this seems to have been occasioned by the baptism of Christ; which the Jew might improve against the disciple of John, and urge, that since another, besides his master, had set up baptizing, who could tell which was most right and safest to follow? and therefore it would have been much better, if no such rite at all had been used by any, but that the purifications required by the law of Moses, and by their elders, had been strictly and solely attended to.
Verse 16
For God sent not his Son into the world,.... God did send his Son into the world in the likeness of sinful flesh, being made of a woman, and made under the law; and which is an instance of his great love, and not of any disrespect to his Son, or of any inequality between them: but then this was not to condemn the world; even any part of it, or any in it: not the Gentiles, as the Jews thought he would; for though God had suffered them to walk in their own ways, and had winked at, or overlooked the times of their ignorance, and had sent no prophet unto them, nor made any revelation of his will, or any discovery of his special grace unto them; yet he sent his Son now, not to destroy them for their idolatry, and wickedness, but to be the Saviour of them: nor the Jews; for as impenitent and unbelieving, and as wicked as they were, he did not accuse them to the Father, nor judge and condemn them; he was to come again in power and great glory, when he would take vengeance on them, and cause wrath to come upon them to the uttermost, for their disbelief and rejection of him; but this was not his business now: nor the wicked of the world in general; to judge, and condemn them, will be his work, when he comes a second time, in the day God has appointed to judge the world in righteousness. But the end of his mission, and first coming is, that the world through him might be saved; even the world of the elect in general, whom God determined to save, and has chosen, to obtain salvation by Jesus Christ, and has appointed Christ to be the salvation of; and who being sent, came into the world to seek and save them; and his chosen people among the Gentiles in particular: wherefore he is said to be God's salvation to the ends of the earth: and all the ends of the earth are called upon to look unto him, and be saved by him, Isa 49:6.
