Commentary Comparison

Tyndale Open Study Notes · Jamieson-Fausset-Brown Bible Commentary · John Gill's Exposition of the Whole Bible · Matthew Henry's Complete Commentary

Points of Agreement

The Tyndale Open Study Notes emphasize that 1 Peter 2 centers on encouraging suffering believers by highlighting their exalted spiritual status as God's temple and covenant people. This pastoral concern unites all commentators. Matthew Henry similarly stresses the apostle's aim to provide comfort through spiritual privileges, noting how Peter describes Christians as "a chosen generation, a royal priesthood" to console Jewish converts worried about losing their special status.

All four commentaries agree on several key interpretative points. They recognize that the "milk of the word" (2:2) refers to God's word generally, not elementary teachings specifically. They interpret the three stone passages (2:6-8) as a unified Old Testament testimony about Christ as cornerstone, with Tyndale noting these quotations were "brought together very early" in Christian tradition. Jamieson-Fausset-Brown and John Gill both emphasize this christological focus, seeing Christ as both foundation and stumbling stone.

Regarding the household code section (2:13-3:7), all commentators acknowledge Peter's practical concern for Christian witness in a hostile environment. Tyndale frames this as living out Gospel principles "so that they can win other people to the Lord," while Henry notes that good behavior may "put to silence the ignorance of foolish men." They also agree that Christian suffering should be patient and Christ-like, with the commentators drawing parallels between servant suffering and Christ's exemplary endurance.

Points of Difference

Tyndale takes a moderate position on predestination in verse 2:8, noting uncertainty about whether people "were appointed by God to unbelief or whether they were, because of their unbelief, appointed by God to suffer condemnation." This contrasts sharply with the other commentaries. John Gill presents a strong Calvinist interpretation, arguing that God "appoints the wicked to punishment, not to crime" but leaves some in disobedience "through which they stumble at Christ." Jamieson-Fausset-Brown offers a middle position, emphasizing God's "penal justice" while maintaining that "the lost shall lay all the blame of their ruin on their own sinful perversity."

The commentaries differ significantly in their treatment of church-state relations. Tyndale provides straightforward historical context about Roman emperors and basic submission principles. However, Gill offers extensive theological reflection on the nature of civil government, distinguishing between divine institution and human forms, while discussing Jewish reluctance to obey Gentile authorities. Matthew Henry provides the most systematic analysis of political obligation, including detailed reasoning about conscience and the limits of Christian liberty.

Their approaches to slavery also vary considerably. Tyndale acknowledges the historical gap, noting that "the slave was legally bound and could not change his or her master, whereas the modern employee has the right to stop working for a cruel employer." The historical commentaries show less awareness of this distinction, with Henry and Gill treating the servant-master relationship as more directly applicable to their contemporary contexts without the same careful qualification Tyndale provides.

Unique Contributions

Tyndale's distinctive offerings include its modern evangelical study Bible approach with practical application bridges. It uniquely addresses the gap between ancient slavery and modern employment, provides clear historical context about Roman imperial structures, and offers balanced theological positions on contested doctrines like predestination. Its transitional notes (like identifying 2:11-12 as potentially concluding the first section or opening the second) show awareness of literary structure that the historical commentaries lack.

Jamieson-Fausset-Brown excels in detailed Greek word studies and cross-biblical connections. Their commentary uniquely explores the nuances of terms like "logikos" (spiritual/reasonable) and provides extensive linguistic analysis of concepts like "living stones." They offer particularly rich intertextual connections, showing how Peter's language relates to Paul's writings and Old Testament passages, creating a web of biblical theology.

John Gill provides unparalleled historical and cultural background, particularly regarding Jewish customs, rabbinic interpretations, and early Christian practices. His commentary uniquely includes extensive citations from Jewish sources, showing how passages like Isaiah 53 were understood messianically. He also offers detailed examination of early Christian persecution and apologetics, quoting figures like Tertullian and Justin Martyr to illuminate the historical context.

Matthew Henry distinguishes himself through systematic practical application and clear moral instruction. His commentary uniquely organizes complex passages into numbered points with clear "Learn" sections that draw practical lessons for Christian living. He excels at connecting theological truths to everyday conduct, offering pastoral wisdom that makes abstract concepts concrete for ordinary believers.

Historical & Theological Context

Tyndale's modern evangelical study Bible approach reflects contemporary hermeneutical awareness and cross-denominational sensitivity. Written for a broad evangelical audience, it avoids denominational distinctives while maintaining conservative theological positions. Its format prioritizes accessibility and practical application, showing influence from modern biblical scholarship's historical-critical methods while maintaining evangelical commitment to scriptural authority.

Jamieson-Fausset-Brown (19th century) represents the height of Protestant scholarly commentary tradition, emphasizing detailed exegetical work and systematic theology. Their approach reflects 19th-century evangelical scholarship's confidence in harmonizing careful textual work with orthodox doctrine. The commentary shows influence from German biblical scholarship while maintaining conservative Reformed theological commitments.

John Gill (18th century) writes from a particular Baptist perspective during the early modern period. His commentary reflects the Puritan tradition's emphasis on detailed verse-by-verse exposition combined with extensive historical learning. His theological framework shows strong Calvinist convictions, and his historical context includes active engagement with Jewish scholarship and early Christian sources that were becoming more available to Protestant scholars.

Matthew Henry (early 18th century) represents the Puritan devotional commentary tradition, writing primarily for ministers and serious lay Bible students. His work reflects the Puritan emphasis on practical godliness and systematic application of Scripture to daily life. His theological context is Reformed but with strong emphasis on experimental religion and personal piety, showing the influence of English Puritanism's concern for both doctrinal orthodoxy and spiritual experience.

Study Questions

Challenge assumptions, probe reasoning, expose tensions

Challenge Assumptions
  1. If believers are 'free' (v.16), why does Peter immediately command submission to human authorities - what kind of freedom is he really describing?
  2. How can Peter simultaneously call believers 'chosen' (v.9) while saying unbelievers were 'destined' to stumble (v.8) - what does this suggest about divine election and human responsibility?
  3. What assumptions about power and authority does Peter challenge when he tells servants to submit even to 'unjust' masters (v.18)?
  4. If Christians are 'sojourners and exiles' (v.11), why should they care about maintaining 'honorable conduct among the Gentiles' (v.12)?
Probe Reasoning
  1. Peter says suffering for doing good is 'gracious' (v.19-20) - what is his underlying logic, and how does this challenge conventional thinking about justice?
  2. Why does Peter connect putting away malice and deceit (v.1) with longing for spiritual milk (v.2) - what is the logical relationship?
  3. How does Peter's reasoning work in verses 11-12: why would abstaining from fleshly passions lead to Gentiles glorifying God?
  4. What chain of reasoning connects being 'healed by his wounds' (v.24) with being able to 'live to righteousness'?
Expose Tensions
  1. How do you resolve the tension between honoring 'everyone' (v.17) and the reality that some people (like unjust masters in v.18) behave dishonorably?
  2. What tension exists between Peter's call for submission (v.13, 18) and Jesus' teaching about not lording authority over others (Mark 10:42-44)?
  3. How can believers be simultaneously 'holy priesthood' (v.5) with direct access to God yet still need to submit to human mediating authorities (v.13-14)?
  4. What tension do you see between the triumphant identity language of verses 9-10 and the suffering described in verses 19-25?

Observe → Interpret → Apply

Observe
  1. What verbs of action does Peter use throughout this chapter (put away, long for, come, abstain, keep, be subject, etc.)?
  2. List all the metaphors Peter uses to describe believers (newborn infants, living stones, royal priesthood, sheep, etc.)
  3. What Old Testament quotations can you identify in verses 6-8, and how does Peter weave them together?
  4. What reasons or motivations does Peter give for his various commands (look for 'for,' 'because,' 'so that' clauses)?
Interpret
  1. Why does Peter move from the metaphor of 'newborn infants' (v.2) to 'living stones' (v.5) - what development is he showing?
  2. What does it mean that some were 'destined' to stumble over the stone (v.8) in light of human responsibility and divine sovereignty?
  3. How does the phrase 'spiritual house' (v.5) relate to the concept of believers as individual 'living stones'?
  4. What is the relationship between suffering unjustly (v.19-20) and following Christ's example (v.21-25)?
Apply
  1. How can you cultivate a 'newborn infant's' appetite for 'pure spiritual milk' in your daily spiritual disciplines?
  2. In what specific relationships or situations is God calling you to follow Christ's example of not reviling in return (v.23)?
  3. How should the reality that you are part of a 'royal priesthood' (v.9) change the way you interact with non-believers this week?
  4. What does it look like practically to 'abstain from the passions of the flesh' (v.11) while living as 'sojourners and exiles'?

Academic approach: genre, history, language, structure

Genre & Structure
  1. How does the paraenetic (ethical instruction) genre of this chapter relate to the preceding theological foundation in 1 Peter 1?
  2. Analyze the chiastic or parallel structure between the identity statements (v.9-10) and the ethical imperatives that follow (v.11-17)
  3. What is the literary function of the servant-master section (v.18-25) within the broader household code structure?
  4. How do the Old Testament quotations in verses 6-8 function as authoritative warrants for Peter's theological argument?
Historical Context
  1. How would Peter's instructions about submission to governors (v.14) have been understood in the context of Roman provincial administration in Asia Minor?
  2. What does the phrase 'day of visitation' (v.12) likely refer to in first-century Jewish and Christian eschatological expectation?
  3. How does the historical context of Christian persecution inform Peter's emphasis on suffering for doing good (v.20)?
  4. What would 'royal priesthood' (v.9) have communicated to Gentile converts unfamiliar with Levitical priesthood?
Word Studies
  1. What is the significance of the Greek word 'lithoi' (living stones) in verse 5, and how does it relate to 'lithos' (stone) describing Christ in verse 4?
  2. Examine the Greek word 'paroikoi' (sojourners) in verse 11: how does this legal/social status inform Peter's ethical instructions?
  3. What does the Greek 'charis' (gracious thing) in verses 19-20 suggest about the nature of undeserved suffering?
  4. How does the Greek construction 'hos kai' (just as, even as) in verse 21 establish the relationship between Christ's suffering and Christian discipleship?
Literary Features
  1. How does Peter's metaphorical progression from 'newborn infants' (v.2) to 'living stones' (v.5) to 'sheep' (v.25) develop his theological argument?
  2. What is the rhetorical effect of Peter's catena (chain) of stone quotations in verses 6-8?
  3. Analyze how the 'once/now' contrast in verse 10 functions as a theological summary of the Christian conversion experience
  4. How does the inclusion of the Christ hymn or creedal material (v.22-25) support Peter's ethical argument about unjust suffering?

Where this fits in God's big story

Salvation Story
  1. How does Peter's description of believers as 'royal priesthood' (v.9) fulfill God's original intention for Israel in Exodus 19:6?
  2. What does the transition from 'not a people' to 'God's people' (v.10) reveal about God's plan to include Gentiles in his covenant family?
  3. How does Christ as the 'living stone' (v.4) represent the culmination of God's promise to establish his dwelling place among his people?
  4. In what ways does verse 24 ('bore our sins in his body on the tree') summarize the substitutionary atonement that resolves the problem introduced in Genesis 3?
Christ Connection
  1. How does Christ's role as both 'cornerstone' and 'stumbling stone' (v.6-8) reveal the dual response to God's salvation throughout biblical history?
  2. What parallels exist between Christ's experience as 'rejected by men but chosen by God' (v.4) and the suffering of Old Testament righteous figures?
  3. How does Christ's example of 'not reviling in return' (v.23) fulfill the Suffering Servant prophecy of Isaiah 53?
  4. In what ways does Christ as 'Shepherd and Overseer' (v.25) fulfill the messianic promises about God shepherding his scattered people?
God's Character
  1. What does God's choice of a 'rejected stone' as the cornerstone (v.7) reveal about his character and values versus human wisdom?
  2. How does God's transformation of 'no people' into 'my people' (v.10) demonstrate his covenant faithfulness despite human unfaithfulness?
  3. What aspects of God's justice are revealed in his call for submission to governing authorities (v.13-14) as part of his ordered creation?
  4. How does God's patient endurance of unjust suffering (modeled by Christ in v.23) reflect his character as 'him who judges justly'?

Jamieson-Fausset-Brown Bible Commentary
Robert Jamieson, A.R. Fausset, David Brown
EXHORTATIONS. (1Pe. 2:1-25) laying aside--once for all: so the Greek aorist expresses as a garment put off. The exhortation applies to Christians alone, for in none else is the new nature existing which, as "the inward man" (Eph 3:16) can cast off the old as an outward thing, so that the Christian, through the continual renewal of his inward man, can also exhibit himself externally as a new man. But to unbelievers the demand is addressed, that inwardly, in regard to the nous (mind), they must become changed, meta-noeisthai (re-pent) [STEIGER]. The "therefore" resumes the exhortation begun in Pe1 1:22. Seeing that ye are born again of an incorruptible seed, be not again entangled in evil, which "has no substantial being, but is an acting in contrariety to the being formed in us" [THEOPHYLACT]. "Malice," &c., are utterly inconsistent with the "love of the brethren," unto which ye have "purified your souls" (Pe1 1:22). The vices here are those which offend against the BROTHERLY LOVE inculcated above. Each succeeding one springs out of that which immediately precedes, so as to form a genealogy of the sins against love. Out of malice springs guile; out of guile, hypocrises (pretending to be what we are not, and not showing what we really are; the opposite of "love unfeigned," and "without dissimulation"); out of hypocrisies, envies of those to whom we think ourselves obliged to play the hypocrite; out of envies, evil-speaking, malicious, envious detraction of others. Guile is the permanent disposition; hypocrisies the acts flowing from it. The guileless knows no envy. Compare Pe1 2:2, "sincere," Greek, "guileless." "Malice delights in another's hurt; envy pines at another's good; guile imparts duplicity to the heart; hypocrisy (flattery) imparts duplicity to the tongue; evil-speakings wound the character of another" [AUGUSTINE].
Verse 24
his own self--there being none other but Himself who could have done it. His voluntary undertaking of the work of redemption is implied. The Greek puts in antithetical juxtaposition, OUR, and His OWN SELF, to mark the idea of His substitution for us. His "well-doing" in His sufferings is set forth here as an example to servants and to us all (Pe1 2:20). bare--to sacrifice: carried and offered up: a sacrificial term. Isa 53:11-12, "He bare the sin of many": where the idea of bearing on Himself is the prominent one; here the offering in sacrifice is combined with that idea. So the same Greek means in Pe1 2:5. our sins--In offering or presenting in sacrifice (as the Greek for "bare" implies) His body, Christ offered in it the guilt of our sins upon the cross, as upon the altar of God, that it might be expiated in Him, and so taken away from us. Compare Isa 53:10, "Thou shalt make His soul an offering for sin." Peter thus means by "bare" what the Syriac takes two words to express, to bear and to offer: (1) He hath borne our sins laid upon Him [namely, their guilt, curse, and punishment]; (2) He hath so borne them that He offered them along with Himself on the altar. He refers to the animals upon which sins were first laid, and which were then offered thus laden [VITRINGA]. Sin or guilt among the Semitic nations is considered as a burden lying heavily upon the sinner [GESENIUS]. on the tree--the cross, the proper place for One on whom the curse was laid: this curse stuck to Him until it was legally (through His death as the guilt-bearer) destroyed in His body: thus the handwriting of the bond against us is cancelled by His death. that we being dead to sins--the effect of His death to "sin" in the aggregate, and to all particular "sins," namely, that we should be as entirely delivered from them, as a slave that is dead is delivered from service to his master. This is our spiritful standing through faith by virtue of Christ's death: our actual mortification of particular sins is in proportion to the degree of our effectually being made conformable to His death. "That we should die to the sins whose collected guilt Christ carried away in His death, and so LIVE TO THE RIGHTEOUSNESS (compare Isa 53:11. 'My righteous servant shall justify many'), the gracious relation to God which He has brought in" [STEIGER]. by whose stripes--Greek, "stripe." ye were healed--a paradox, yet true. "Ye servants (compare 'buffeted,' 'the tree,' Pe1 2:20, Pe1 2:24) often bear the strife; but it is not more than your Lord Himself bore; learn from Him patience in wrongful sufferings.
Verse 25
(Isa 53:6.) For--Assigning their natural need of healing (Pe1 2:24). now--Now that the atonement for all has been made, the foundation is laid for individual conversion: so "ye are returned," or "have become converted to," &c. Shepherd and Bishop--The designation of the pastors and elders of the Church belongs in its fullest sense to the great Head of the Church, "the good Shepherd." As the "bishop" oversees (as the Greek term means), so "the eyes of the Lord are over the righteous" (Pe1 3:12). He gives us His spirit and feeds and guides us by His word. "Shepherd," Hebrew, "Parnas," is often applied to kings, and enters into the composition of names, as "Pharnabazus." Next: 1 Peter Chapter 3

John Gill's Exposition of the Whole Bible
John Gill
INTRODUCTION TO 1 PETER 2 This chapter consists of exhortations, in general, to a holy life; and, in particular, to obedience to superiors. It begins with an exhortation to lay aside various vices, which were unbecoming regenerate persons, Pe1 2:1 and, agreeably to their character, as new born babes, to express a desire after the Gospel, which is commended from its nature, being comparable to milk; and from its quality, being reasonable, sincere, and unmixed; and from the end and usefulness of it, a spiritual growth; and the argument engaging to such a desire is the experience they had of the grace of Christ in it, Pe1 2:2 whose excellency is declared, under the metaphor of a stone, said to be living, to be chosen of God, and precious to him, though rejected by men; to whom the saints are encouraged to come, as lively stones built up, a spiritual house, for the exercise of the holy office of the priesthood, by offering up spiritual sacrifices acceptable to God through Christ, Pe1 2:4 and that Christ is such a precious stone, is proved from Isa 28:16 and not only to God, but to all them that believe; though to them that believe not, he is the stone of rejection, stumbling, and offence; to stumble at which, and the doctrine of it, through disobedience, they were of old appointed by God, Pe1 2:6 but the character of the persons the apostle writes to was very different, quite the reverse, being chosen and called, and manifestly the people of God, and sharing in his grace and mercy, Pe1 2:9 and these he exhorts, suitable to their state and condition, to abstain from sin, as an enemy to their souls, and to live an honest life and conversation among the Gentiles, that they, instead of speaking evil of them, might, by beholding their good works, glorify God, Pe1 2:11. And particularly he exhorts them to obedience to civil magistrates, both superior and inferior; partly from the author of them, they being of the Lord; and partly from the usefulness of them to punish wicked...
Verse 24
For ye were as sheep going astray,.... This is a proof of their being healed, namely, their conversion; in which an application of the blood of Christ, and pardon, and so healing by it, was made to their souls. The apostle has still in view the prophecy of Isa 53:6. God's elect are sheep before conversion; not that they have the agreeable properties of sheep, as to be meek, harmless, innocent, clean, and profitable, for they are the reverse of all this; nor can some things be said of them before conversion, as may be after, as that they hear Christ's voice, and follow him; nor are they so called, because unprejudiced against, and predisposed unto the Gospel, for the contrary is true of them; but they are so in electing grace, and were so considered in the Father's gift of them to Christ, and when made his care and charge, and hence they are called the sheep of his hand; and when Christ laid down his life, and rose again, which he did for the sheep, and as the great Shepherd of them; and when called by grace, for their being sheep, and Christ's own sheep by the Father's gift, and his own purpose, is the reason why he looks them up, calls them by name, and returns them: but then they are not yet of his fold; they are lost sheep, lost in Adam, and by his fall, and by their own actual transgressions; they are as sheep going astray from the shepherd, and from the flock, going out of the right way, and in their own ways; and are, like sheep, stupid and insensible of their danger; and as they never return of themselves, until they are sought for, and brought back: hence it follows, but are now returned; not returned themselves, but were returned by powerful and efficacious grace: saints are passive, and not active in first conversion; they are turned, not by the power of their own free will, but by the power of God's free grace; they are returned under the illuminations and quickenings of the blessed Spirit, and through the efficacious drawings of the Father's love, unto Christ: unto the Shepherd and Bishop of your souls; by whom Christ is meant, who bears the office of a Shepherd, and fully performs it by feeding his sheep, providing a good fold and pasture for them; by gathering the lambs in his arms, and gently leading those that are with young; by healing their diseases, and preserving them from beasts of prey; hence he is called the good, the great, and chief Shepherd: and he is the "Bishop" or "Overseer" of the souls of his people, though not to the exclusion of their bodies: he has took the oversight of them willingly, and looks well to his flock, inspects into their cases, and often visits them, and never forsakes them; nor will he leave them till they receive the end of their faith, the salvation of their souls; which he has undertook and effected by his obedience, sufferings and death. Philo the Jew (l) observes, that "to be a shepherd is so good a work, that it is not only a title given to kings and wise men, and souls perfectly purified, but to God the governor of all---who, as a Shepherd and King, leads according to justice and law, setting over them his right Logos, "the first begotten Son", who has taken the care of this holy flock, as does the deputy of a great king. (l) De Agricultura, p. 194, 195. Next: 1 Peter Chapter 3
Matthew Henry's Complete Commentary
Matthew Henry
The general exhortation to holiness is continued, and enforced by several reasons taken from the foundation on which Christians are built, Jesus Christ, and from their spiritual blessings and privileges in him. The means of obtaining it, the word of God, is recommended, and all contrary qualities are condemned (Pe1 2:1-12). Particular directions are given how subjects ought to obey the magistrates, and servants their masters, patiently suffering in well doing, in imitation of Christ (Pe1 2:13 to the end).
